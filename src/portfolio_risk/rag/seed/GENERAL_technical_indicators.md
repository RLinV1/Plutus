# Common chart metrics explained (plain English)

This explains the metrics people see on stock charts. It is educational
information, not investment advice. These tools describe the past; they cannot
predict the future.

## Moving averages (the 50-day and 200-day)
A moving average smooths out the daily noise to show the underlying trend. The
**200-day moving average** is just the average closing price over the last 200
trading days; the **50-day moving average** is the average over the last 50 days.

How people read them:
- When the price is **above** its 200-day average, many investors describe the
  stock as being in a long-term **uptrend**.
- When the price is **below** its 200-day average, they call it a long-term
  **downtrend**.
- The 50-day average reacts faster than the 200-day because it covers a shorter
  window.

## Golden cross and death cross
When the faster 50-day average crosses **above** the slower 200-day average, it is
nicknamed a **golden cross** and is often seen as a bullish (positive) signal.
When the 50-day crosses **below** the 200-day, it is called a **death cross** and
is seen as bearish (negative). These are popular rules of thumb, not guarantees.

## RSI (Relative Strength Index)
RSI is a momentum gauge from 0 to 100 that summarizes how quickly a stock has been
rising or falling recently. A common convention: above 70 is called "overbought"
(it has risen quickly and might pause), and below 30 is called "oversold" (it has
fallen quickly and might bounce). Around 50 is considered neutral.

## 52-week high and low
This is simply the highest and lowest price over the past year. Knowing whether a
stock is near its 52-week high or low tells you where today's price sits within
its recent range.

## A caution
All of these are descriptive tools based on past prices. They are widely watched,
but no indicator reliably predicts what a price will do next.
