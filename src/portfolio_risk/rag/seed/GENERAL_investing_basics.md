# Stock investing basics (plain English)

This is a beginner explainer. It is educational information, not investment advice.

## What is a stock?
A stock (or "share") is a small piece of ownership in a company. If a company is
divided into a million shares and you own one, you own a millionth of the company.
When the company does well and more people want to own it, the price of a share
tends to rise; when people are worried, it tends to fall.

## What is the share price?
The price is simply what buyers and sellers are currently willing to trade a
single share for. It changes constantly during market hours. A high price per
share does not mean a company is "expensive" overall — you have to look at the
whole company's value.

## What is market capitalization (market cap)?
Market cap is the total value of all a company's shares added together: share
price multiplied by the number of shares. It tells you how big the company is.
Rough buckets people use: small-cap (under ~$2 billion), mid-cap (~$2–10 billion),
and large-cap (over ~$10 billion). The very largest companies are worth more than
a trillion dollars.

## What is the P/E ratio?
P/E stands for price-to-earnings. It compares the share price to how much profit
the company earns per share in a year. A P/E of 25 roughly means investors are
paying $25 for every $1 of yearly earnings. A higher P/E often means investors
expect strong future growth; a lower P/E can mean the company is cheaper or that
people are less optimistic. P/E is most useful when comparing similar companies.

## What is volatility?
Volatility measures how much a stock's price bounces around. A "calm" stock moves
a little day to day; a "bumpy" stock can swing a lot. More volatility means bigger
ups *and* bigger downs — more potential reward, but also more potential loss.

## Why do prices move?
Prices move on news, earnings reports, interest rates, the overall economy, and
plain old supply and demand. Nobody can reliably predict short-term moves.
